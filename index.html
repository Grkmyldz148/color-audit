The Open-Source Color Audit

You call it blue‑500. Is it?

We measured the color systems everyone copies — Tailwind, Material, Bootstrap, Primer, Radix, Chakra. Here's what your tokens actually do.

How to read this page

Every swatch here is the real, untouched color from each system's published package.

We measured how different neighboring shades actually look — not how different their names sound — and turned the results into pictures you can judge with your own eyes.

Headlines say what breaks; the small print underneath carries the numbers, and the full table waits at the bottom.

Finding 01 · Material Design

Two tokens. Basically one color.

Material's blue-400 and blue-500 are officially two different design tokens. To the eye, they're almost the same paint.

Spot the boundary. Somewhere in this strip, 400 becomes 500.

Material blue 400 vs 500 — this is your button and its hover state. If your hover feedback, your selected-vs-unselected tab, or your chart series relies on this pair, most people will never see the difference. Meanwhile the jump from 50 to 100, in the same ramp, is 6× bigger.

Perceptual distance 400→500 = 0.026 (helmlab GenSpace) — the smallest chromatic step measured in this audit. 50→100 = 0.162.

Finding 02 · Bootstrap

One step whispers, another shouts.

Below, each gap is drawn exactly as wide as the measured perceptual distance between neighbors. Even scale, even gaps. This is Bootstrap's blue.

The 100→200 jump is 8× the 400→500 jump — Bootstrap blue. The token names promise a staircase with even steps; what ships is one cliff at the light end and three near-flat steps in the middle. A "one shade darker" tweak means almost nothing at 400 and a completely different color at 100.

100→200 = 0.303, 400→500 = 0.038 in GenSpace. Bootstrap tints and shades by mixing RGB with white/black — equal paint-mixing is not equal seeing.

Finding 03 · GitHub Primer

You call it blue. The dark end disagrees.

Primer's blue ramp doesn't just get darker — it quietly changes hue on the way down. To prove it's hue and nothing else, we re-rendered the ramp's first and last hue at identical lightness and colorfulness. Everything you see differing below is hue drift alone.

That's 27.6° of hue drift — the largest we measured. The light end of Primer's blue leans cyan; the dark end leans violet. Ship a "blue" brand, tint your dark surfaces with blue-8, and you're tinting with a different color than your marketing blue.

Hues 232.3° and 259.9° from Primer blue steps 0 and 8, both re-rendered at GenSpace L=0.6, C=0.2 via helmlab genFromLch → #00a1cd vs #5b94de.

Finding 04 · Tailwind CSS

The two shades you use most are the two closest together.

In countless Tailwind codebases, blue-500 is the button and blue-600 is its hover. They are the closest pair in the entire ramp.

The 200→300 jump is 4.9× the 500→600 jump. A subtle hover is a legitimate design choice — just know the ramp made that choice for you, and users with low-end screens or mild color vision deficiency may not get any hover feedback at all. Red has the same squeeze, plus one huge cliff into 950.

500→600 = 0.045, 200→300 = 0.219. Tailwind red 900→950 = 0.318, the ramp's biggest jump.

Finding 05 · GitHub Primer

One gray scale. Except it's two.

Primer's 14-step neutral behaves like two scales glued together: seven near-identical light grays, a cliff, then a normal dark ramp.

Pick "the next gray down" near the top and nothing visibly changes; do it at the 7→8 seam and everything does. That jump is 19× the 4→5 jump. This isn't necessarily wrong — light-mode UIs need many whisper-quiet surface grays — but if you're choosing border and divider colors by nudging the step number, this ramp will not behave linearly.

4→5 = 0.011, 7→8 = 0.206 in GenSpace. The gaps above are the measured distances, to scale.

Finding 06 · Material Design

It stops getting darker halfway down.

Strip the color away and render each token of Material's red as neutral gray at its measured lightness — the ramp's darkness skeleton. Top row: what ships. Bottom row: how dark each step actually is.

Same step, different darkness jump. Material red darkens fast, then almost stalls: from 500 to 900 the skeleton barely moves. White text that passes on red-600 will behave almost identically on red-800 — and if your error-state hierarchy expects "darker = more severe," the bottom half of this ramp isn't delivering it.

Measured GenSpace L: 500 = 0.58, 700 = 0.49, 900 = 0.42. Lightness-step consistency (L-CV) 65.5% — the most uneven chromatic darkness ladder in the audit. Grays rendered via helmlab genFromLch([L, 0, 0]).

Finding 07 · Credit where due

Tailwind's blue never stops being blue.

Same test we ran on Primer: first-step hue vs worst-drift hue, re-rendered at identical lightness and colorfulness. Try to tell them apart.

That's 9.6° of drift across eleven steps — you can barely see it, and that's the point. This is Tailwind's v4 move to designing ramps in a perceptual color space paying off: dark blue is still the same blue, so tinted dark surfaces, focus rings and charts stay on-brand at every step.

Hues 257.6° and 267.2° from Tailwind blue 100 and 950, rendered at GenSpace L=0.6, C=0.2 → #5595dd vs #6b90de. Tailwind gray drifts just 6.9°.

Finding 08 · Credit where due

Primer red: the most even ramp we measured.

This is what the gap test looks like when someone engineered the ramp. Every "+1" means roughly the same thing to the eye.

Every jump lands between 0.107 and 0.193 — the biggest step is only 1.8× the smallest, the tightest spread of any scale in the audit. You can pick any two neighbors for a state change and trust them to read as "one step apart." Primer's blue and green are nearly as disciplined, and all three mid shades hold white text.

Step consistency (CV) 16.9%, max/min 1.8×. Primer mids vs white: blue 5.19:1, red 5.36:1, green 5.08:1 — all ≥ 4.5:1 (WCAG AA).

Finding 09 · Everyone passes

Nobody shipped a dead zone.

Every system's flagship mid blue, wearing whichever text color measures better on it.

Every system's mid shade is usable on white or black — each one holds at least 4.5:1 contrast against one of them, so there's always a legible text pairing. And every one of the 24 scales gets strictly darker as the numbers go up: zero lightness reversals across all 228 steps. The basics, everywhere, are sound.

mid = step 500 (Radix: step 9, Primer: step 5). WCAG contrast vs #fff and #000; "fails both" count = 0 for all six systems. Monotonicity violations = 0 across 228 steps.

Finding 10 · Radix Colors

Radix comes last. The ranking is the wrong lens.

Run the gap test on Radix and it looks broken — tiny steps, then a canyon. It isn't broken. It's a different kind of system.

Radix scores "worst" only because its 12 steps are jobs, not a ramp — steps 1–2 are backgrounds, 3–5 component states, 9 is the solid color, 11–12 are text. Judged as a ramp, that's the wrong lens; that canyon before step 12 is the deliberate contrast between text and everything else — the largest single step in the audit, on purpose. We show it for completeness: if you need an even ramp, Radix never promised you one. If you need steps that map to UI jobs, this is the point.

Radix red 11→12 = 0.435 — the audit's largest step. Rescored on interior steps 2–11 only, blue and green become ordinary (step CV 30.8% / 37.3%); red (64.6%) and gray (97.6%) stay uneven either way.

Methodology

Every hex token is mapped into helmlab GenSpace (hl.genFromHex(hex) → [L, a, b] with L in 0–1; hl.genToLch → [L, C, h°]), a perceptually uniform generation space. Four measurements per scale, plus two visual-proof constructions:

1 · Step-distance consistency (step CV)

d_i = GenSpace Euclidean distance(token_i, token_{i+1}) step CV = std(d) / mean(d) × 100 (plus max/min ratio)

Lower CV = more even perceptual steps. The proportional-gap rows draw these distances directly: the gap between two swatches is proportional to d_i within that row.

2 · Lightness uniformity (L-CV) and monotonicity

dL_i = L_{i+1} − L_i L-CV = std(dL) / |mean(dL)| × 100 (any sign flip → monotonicity violation)

3 · Hue drift

drift = max_i |h_i − h_first| with 360° wrap, skipping steps with C < 0.03 as achromatic

4 · Contrast

WCAG contrastRatio(mid, #ffffff) and contrastRatio(mid, #000000) flag if both < 4.5 (mid = 500, Radix step 9, Primer step 5)

5 · The hue-proof swatches (Findings 03 & 07)

h_start = hue of first non-achromatic step, h_end = hue at the worst-drift step swatch = hl.genFromLch([0.6, 0.2, h]) → hex (identical L and C for both; C reduced jointly if either hue falls outside sRGB at C = 0.2)

Because both swatches share the same GenSpace lightness and chroma, any visible difference between them is hue drift and nothing else. Both pairs shown on this page are in-gamut at C = 0.2.

6 · The lightness skeleton (Finding 06 and every report card)

gray_i = hl.genFromLch([L_i, 0, 0]) → hex (L_i = the token's measured GenSpace L)

Rendering each token as a neutral gray at its measured lightness strips hue and chroma away, leaving only the darkness ladder — uneven lightness becomes visible without a chart.

Provenance & limitations

  • Token sources are the official npm packages, versions recorded in data/tokens/*.json. Tailwind v4 publishes oklch(), converted to sRGB hex via culori; Bootstrap's chromatic ramps are generated exactly per its own tint-color/shade-color (sass mix()) definitions.
  • This audit measures scale quality only — how evenly and predictably a ramp is spaced in a perceptual space. It says nothing about overall design quality, aesthetics, component design, or the fitness of these palettes for their intended workflows.
  • Radix Colors is role-based by design: its 12 steps are documented use-case roles, not an even ramp, so uniformity metrics measure a contract it never signed. See Finding 10 and its card.
  • Results depend on the choice of perceptual space; GenSpace correlates strongly with OKLab-class spaces, so orderings should be broadly stable, but exact numbers are space-specific.
  • This page checks itself: at build time, every text/background pair in the design is verified against WCAG (body text ≥ 7:1 AAA, secondary text ≥ 4.5:1 AA) using helmlab's contrastRatio — the build fails otherwise.
