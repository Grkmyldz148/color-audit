The Open-Source Color Audit

You call it blue‑500. Is it?

We measured the color systems everyone copies — Tailwind, Material, Bootstrap, Primer, Radix, Chakra. Here's what your tokens actually do.

How to read this page

Every swatch here is the real, untouched color from each system's published package. We measured how the tokens actually look — not how their names sound — and turned the results into pictures you can judge with your own eyes.

The findings come in five chapters, each a different question a designer actually asks:

  1. Steps — does "+1" always mean one visual step?
  2. Hue — is your blue still blue? Is your gray even gray?
  3. Weight — do same-numbered tokens carry the same weight, and what does that do to contrast?
  4. What to steal — the one measured trait each system does best.
  5. If we built one — a palette generated from the math, audited by its own pipeline.

Headlines say what breaks; the small print underneath carries the numbers, and the full table waits at the bottom.

Chapter 1 · Steps

A “+1” should always buy the same amount of change.

Token names promise a staircase — every step down the scale should feel like one step. We measured the actual size of all 228 steps. The gaps in the pictures below are drawn exactly as wide as the measured change.

1.1 · Bootstrap

One step whispers, another shouts.

Below, each gap is drawn exactly as wide as the measured perceptual distance between neighbors. Even scale, even gaps. This is Bootstrap's blue.

The 100→200 jump is 8× the 400→500 jump. The token names promise even stairs; what ships is one cliff at the light end and three whisper-small steps in the middle. The same "one shade darker" tweak buys 8× more visible change at 100 than at 400.

100→200 = 0.303, 400→500 = 0.038 in GenSpace. Bootstrap tints and shades by mixing RGB with white/black — equal paint-mixing is not equal seeing.

1.2 · GitHub Primer

One gray scale. Except it's two.

Primer's 14-step neutral behaves like two scales glued together: seven whisper-quiet light grays, a cliff, then a normal dark ramp. How quiet is the quiet end? The second strip answers: gray 4 meets gray 5 at its midpoint, no seam drawn.

The 7→8 seam is 19× the 4→5 step — and 4→5 is the only pair among all 228 steps that measures genuinely near threshold (0.019, under this build's 0.025 near-duplicate gate), which is why it alone gets the spot-the-boundary strip. Every other "closest pair" in the audit is plainly visible butted together, and showing one this way would falsify the claim on sight. Many quiet surface grays isn't necessarily wrong for light-mode UI — but if you pick borders by nudging the step number, this ramp will not behave linearly.

4→5 = 0.011, 7→8 = 0.206 in GenSpace; gray 4 = #e0e6eb, gray 5 = #dae0e7, trained-metric difference 0.019 on a scale that saturates near 0.15 (black↔white). Build gate: any spot-the-boundary visual must measure < 0.025 or the build fails — see methodology.

1.3 · Material & Tailwind

The smallest steps hide on the button hover.

Material's blue, gap-spaced by measured distance: a 6× leap from 50 to 100, while blue-400 → blue-500 — in countless UIs a button and its hover — is the smallest chromatic step in the entire audit. Tailwind makes the same trade on the exact same slot: blue-500 → blue-600 is the closest pair in its ramp.

The chips are the receipts. Both pairs are visibly different — design systems make steps visible on purpose — the finding is where each ramp chose to put its least contrast: on the state change your users are supposed to notice, where an in-place change is always easier to miss than two chips side by side. It doesn't have to be this way: Primer's red lands every jump between 0.107 and 0.193 (biggest step only 1.8× the smallest) — the most even hand-built ramp we measured. It gets its flowers in Chapter 4.

Material blue 400→500 = 0.026 — the audit's smallest chromatic step (asserted at build time) — vs 50→100 = 0.162 (6×). Tailwind blue 500→600 = 0.045 vs 200→300 = 0.219 (4.9×). For scale, the trained difference metric rates the pairs 0.044 and 0.072 on a scale saturating near 0.15 — both well above threshold, which is why they get receipt chips, never a spot-the-boundary strip.

Chapter 2 · Hue

Is your blue still blue? Is your gray even gray?

Chapter 1 measured how far apart the steps are. This chapter measures what color they actually are — because a ramp can drift away from its own name on the way down, a "gray" can secretly carry a tint, and six systems can disagree about what blue-500 even means.

2.1 · Primer vs Tailwind

You call it blue. The dark end disagrees.

A ramp shouldn't just get darker — it should stay the color on its name tag. To test that, we took each blue ramp's first and worst-drift hues and re-rendered both at identical lightness and colorfulness: everything you see differing below is hue drift alone. First pair: Primer. Second pair: Tailwind, same test.

Primer drifts 27.6° — the largest we measured: the light end leans cyan, the dark end leans violet. Ship a "blue" brand, tint your dark surfaces with blue-8, and you're tinting with a different color than your marketing blue. Tailwind, on the same test, drifts 9.6° — roughly a third — the visible payoff of v4's move to designing ramps in a perceptual color space: dark blue is still recognizably the same blue, so tinted surfaces, focus rings and charts stay on-brand at every step.

Primer hues 232.3° / 259.9° (steps 0→8); Tailwind 257.6° / 267.2° (100→950). All four swatches rendered at GenSpace L=0.6, C=0.2 via helmlab genFromLch — identical lightness and chroma, so only hue differs. Tailwind gray drifts just 6.9°.

2.2 · All six systems

Your gray isn't gray.

We measured the colorfulness (GenSpace chroma) of every gray token in all six systems — a true neutral measures C = 0. Below, each system's most-tinted gray sits next to a true neutral rebuilt at the exact same lightness. Any difference you can see is tint, nothing else.

Two systems ship ink-true grays: Material and Radix measure C = 0.000 at every step — their pairs above are literally identical chips. The rest lean cool: Tailwind's gray-900 (C 0.063) and Chakra's gray-500 (C 0.063) are in a dead heat for the most tinted "gray" in the audit — more than twice the audit's own neutrality cutoff — with Primer's gray-8 behind them (C 0.038). Bootstrap's faint lean (C 0.027) stays under the cutoff. And not one gray token anywhere in the audit leans warm: when design systems tint gray, they tint it blue. That's a defensible taste — cool grays read "clean screen" — but if your product mixes these tokens with a true neutral (a photo border, a #808080 from anywhere else), the seam will show.

C = genToLch(genFromHex(hex))[1] per gray token; tinted = C ≥ 0.03 (the audit's achromatic cutoff); neutral twin = genFromLch([measured L, 0, 0]). Most-tinted per system: Chakra 500 C 0.0626 (hue 261°), Tailwind 900 C 0.0626 (263°), Primer 8 C 0.0376 (259°), Bootstrap 600 C 0.0269 (249°), Material and Radix 0.0000 everywhere. All claims asserted at build time, including "every tinted gray is cool" (hue 220–280°).

2.3 · All six systems

There is no such thing as blue-500.

Six systems, one token name — the mid blue every brand reaches for. Here they all are, sorted darkest to lightest. Same name; six different colors. This is the one visual on the page that cannot be argued with: the differences are the point.

The name "blue-500" specifies neither a hue nor a weight: across these six tokens the hue spans 254.2°–262.6° (8.4° apart — Material Design and Chakra lean cyan, Tailwind and Bootstrap lean violet-blue) and the lightness spans 0.464–0.599 — Material's mid blue is 29% lighter than Primer's. Port a design between systems, or copy a component from a codebase that uses a different one, and "just use blue-500" silently changes both the color and the weight of everything it touches. The contrast bill for that lands in Chapter 3.

Mid steps per system: 500 (Tailwind, Material, Bootstrap, Chakra), 9 (Radix), 5 (Primer) — each system's own documented solid/mid shade. L and hue via genToLch; white-text contrast ranges 3.12:1–5.19:1 across the six. Build asserts every pair of the six measures well above the near-duplicate threshold (visibly different) and that the L spread stays material (> 0.1).

Chapter 3 · Weight

Same number, different weight.

Within one system, blue-500, red-500, green-500 and gray-500 share a step number — so designers treat them as interchangeable weights. We measured whether they actually are, and what that does to your text contrast.

3.1 · All six systems

Tokens with the same name don't carry the same visual weight.

Each system's mid row — blue, red, green, gray at the same step — with every chip's neutral-gray twin fused underneath at its measured lightness. If the four gray twins in a row match, the row carries one weight. Rows are sorted, tightest first.

Only Radix genuinely delivers "same step, same weight": its four solids sit within 0.028 L of each other — the tightest row we measured, and a direct payoff of designing steps as roles. Chakra is close (0.045). Then it falls apart: Tailwind's green-500 floats 0.21 L above its own gray-500, Bootstrap's gray-500 is a pale outlier in an otherwise matched row (spread 0.254), and Primer's "gray 5" isn't a mid-tone at all — it lives at L 0.89, a souvenir of the glued scale from Chapter 1. Swap one 500 for another inside these systems and your design silently changes weight.

Mid-row lightness spread (max − min GenSpace L across blue/red/green/gray at the mid step): Radix 0.028, Chakra 0.045, Material 0.067, Tailwind 0.207, Bootstrap 0.254, Primer 0.447. Twins rendered via genFromLch([L, 0, 0]). Build asserts Radix's row is the tightest and Chakra's second.

3.2 · The accessibility bill

White text passes on one 500 and fails on another.

The consequence designers feel immediately: the same white label on every system's mid row, with the measured WCAG ratio. Normal text needs 4.5:1 (AA). Every ratio below is computed, not estimated.

Same step number, opposite verdicts — inside a single system: white text passes on Tailwind's gray-500 at 4.84:1 and fails on its green-500 at 2.22:1. Across systems it's worse: a white label that clears AA on Primer's blue (5.19:1) fails on Material's blue-500 (3.12:1) — same token name, opposite audit result. Primer is the only system whose blue, red and green mids all clear AA with room (5.08–5.36:1); Bootstrap's three color mids scrape past (4.50–4.53:1) while its gray fails at 2.07:1; Material's and Radix's mid rows hold white text nowhere. A step number is not a contrast guarantee — if your button component assumes "white text on any 500," it is already broken somewhere.

contrastRatio('#ffffff', hex) per mid token via helmlab; PASS = ≥ 4.5:1 (WCAG AA, normal text). Every PASS/FAIL badge above is asserted at build time — including the borderline one: Bootstrap blue passes at 4.5008:1, a margin of 0.0008. Radix documents its step 9 for both light and dark text depending on the scale; the measurement here is white text only. Black text rescues most failures — see the closing chapter.

Chapter 5 · If we built one

The math is not a secret.

Everything above is measurable — which means it's buildable. So we built one: three seed colors in, a full palette out, generated at build time by helmlab's semanticScale (no hex in this chapter is hand-picked), then pushed through the exact pipeline that audited the six systems. What follows is the output, its scores — wins and losses alike — and the five formulas it obeys. Steal the principles; the library is optional.

5.2 · The blueprint

Five principles, five receipts.

Each principle is one line of math; each receipt is measured from the palette above — the same one in the table. None of this needs helmlab specifically: any perceptual color space with a lightness/chroma/hue form and a gamut mapping that holds hue can implement all five.

1 · The lightness ladder

L_i = ladder(i) in perceptual L — never a % of white or black paint mixed in
blue — generatedstep CV 59.9% · L-CV 25.8% · hue drift 1.9°
gray — generatedstep CV 47.4% · L-CV 25.1% · C 0.0000 everywhere

The ramp is designed top-down in seen lightness: the gray strip is the blue ramp's own ladder with the color removed, and the two match step for step because gray was built from blue's measured L. Bootstrap mixes paint instead — equal RGB percentages — and Chapter 1.1 showed the cliff that buys. Honesty note: placed in L is not the same as perfectly even in L. The generated ladder eases at both ends and runs two near-constant bands in the middle (≈0.085 L per step in the light half, ≈0.113 in the dark half); its mean L-CV of 30.7% ranks 3rd — Bootstrap's machine mixing (24.1%) and Chakra (28.2%) are tighter. The principle buys a ladder that means something to the eye; it doesn't automatically win the evenness column.

2 · The hue lock

h_i = h_seed for every i — down the ramp, only L and C are allowed to move

This is Chapter 2.1's test run on ourselves: the generated blue's first-step hue and worst-drift hue, re-rendered at identical lightness and chroma and butted together with no seam drawn. The pair measures 0.0121 — under the page's own 0.025 near-duplicate gate, the same gate only one shipped pair in the whole audit (Primer gray 4→5) passed — so the seamless strip is earned, not rhetorical. Our worst ramp is red at 2.7°, whose equal-L/C pair measures 0.0297 — a hair above the gate, so it doesn't get the seamless treatment; that's the gate working. For scale: the best audited system allows 11.8° somewhere in its palette, Primer 27.6°. Asserted at build time: every generated ramp holds under 3°.

3 · Chroma respects the gamut

C_i = min(C_seed, maxC(L_i, h)) — clip chroma, never rotate hue

Filled bars: the measured chroma of each generated blue step. Dashed outlines: the most chroma sRGB can physically show at that step's lightness and this hue. Gold dashes: the seed's chroma. The filled bar always hugs whichever bound is lower — measured within 0.006 of min(seed, ceiling) at all 11 steps, asserted under 0.025 on all three ramps. This one formula is where much of Chapter 2's hue drift comes from when it's done wrong: a dark step wants more chroma than sRGB has at that lightness, something must give, and clipping in RGB gives up hue — that's how dark blues turn violet. The right thing to surrender is chroma.

4 · Gray means gray

gray_i = LCh [L_i, 0, 0] — chroma is zero by construction, on the blue ramp's ladder

Chapter 2.2's test, run on ourselves: the worst chroma across all eleven generated grays is 0.0000, so the shipped chip and its rebuilt neutral twin above are literally the same color — the property only Material and Radix ship, and the one every other audited system trades away for a cool tint. You don't earn this gray by tuning; you get it free the moment gray is defined as C = 0 instead of "blue with the chroma turned down."

5 · Steps clear the JND

min_i difference(step_i, step_i+1) ≥ 0.025 — every adjacent pair, checked with this page's own gate

Every adjacent pair in all four generated scales is checked against the same 0.025 near-duplicate threshold the honesty gate uses — asserted at build time. The chips above are our closest pair anywhere, gray 50→100, at 0.046 (1.8× the gate); the closest chromatic pair measures 0.059. Honest context from Chapter 1.3: Material's blue 400→500 hover step measures 0.044 in the same units — our floor is about the same size as theirs. The difference is placement: ours sits at the extreme pale end of the gray ramp, not on the button-hover slot where a state change has to register.

5.3 · What the math doesn't buy

A generator gives you geometry, not judgment.

Four things this chapter's math will not hand you. Hand-tuned optical corrections — Material's 2014 palette was adjusted shade by shade by human eyes, and Chapter 4 showed hand-tuning can beat generation on evenness (Primer's red at step CV 16.9% beats every generated ramp here). Brand voice — a generator anchors your seed faithfully; it cannot tell you whether your seed is right, and it inherits your seed's flaws: our green-500 fails white text at 2.28:1, almost exactly like the Tailwind green it resembles (2.22:1), because generation preserves the seed by design. Role semantics — Radix's steps name jobs (backgrounds, states, text; Chapter 4), and no formula assigns your team's meanings to a dial. And the last word in near-neutral ramps — helmlab's own published benchmark keeps honesty tables where OKLab-family spaces score slightly better on near-achromatic steps; if your palette lives close to gray, an OKLab implementation of these same five formulas is a fine choice (helmlab.space/benchmark). The principles are space-portable. That's the point: steal the principles, not necessarily the library.

Methodology

Every hex token is mapped into helmlab GenSpace (hl.genFromHex(hex) → [L, a, b] with L in 0–1; hl.genToLch → [L, C, h°]), a perceptually uniform generation space. Four measurements per scale, plus the visual-proof constructions below.

1 · Step-distance consistency (step CV) — Chapter 1

d_i = GenSpace Euclidean distance(token_i, token_{i+1}) step CV = std(d) / mean(d) × 100 (plus max/min ratio)

Lower CV = more even perceptual steps. The proportional-gap rows draw these distances directly: the gap between two swatches is proportional to d_i within that row.

2 · Lightness uniformity (L-CV) and monotonicity

dL_i = L_{i+1} − L_i L-CV = std(dL) / |mean(dL)| × 100 (any sign flip → monotonicity violation)

3 · Hue drift and the hue-proof swatches — Chapter 2.1

drift = max_i |h_i − h_first| with 360° wrap, skipping steps with C < 0.03 as achromatic proof swatch = hl.genFromLch([0.6, 0.2, h]) → hex (identical L and C for both hues; C reduced jointly if either hue falls outside sRGB at C = 0.2)

Because both swatches share the same GenSpace lightness and chroma, any visible difference between them is hue drift and nothing else. All pairs shown on this page are in-gamut at C = 0.2.

4 · Gray-tint measurement — Chapter 2.2

C = genToLch(genFromHex(gray_token))[1] for every gray token in every system tinted ⇔ C ≥ 0.03 (the audit's achromatic cutoff) neutral twin = genFromLch([measured L, 0, 0]) → hex

A true neutral measures C = 0; its twin is then the identical color, which is what the Material and Radix pairs show. The build asserts the headline claims: Material and Radix at C < 0.001 on every step, the Tailwind-900/Chakra-500 dead heat (ΔC < 0.001, both ≥ 0.05), and that every tinted gray token in the audit leans cool (hue 220–280°).

5 · The mid-shade lineup — Chapter 2.3

mid = each system's documented solid/mid step (500; Radix 9; Primer 5) per token: L, hue via genToLch; pairwise hl.difference across the six blues

7 · The lightness skeleton — Chapters 3 & 4

gray_i = hl.genFromLch([L_i, 0, 0]) → hex (L_i = the token's measured GenSpace L)

Rendering each token as a neutral gray at its measured lightness strips hue and chroma away, leaving only the darkness ladder — uneven lightness becomes visible without a chart.

8 · The near-duplicate honesty gate — Chapter 1.2

spot-the-boundary / seamless side-by-side visual ⇒ hl.difference(pair) < 0.025 otherwise the BUILD FAILS

Visual proof patterns are type-checked against the claim they make: a visual that invites you to struggle to see a difference ("spot the boundary") is only honest if the measured difference is genuinely sub-threshold — being a ramp's smallest step does not make a pair perceptually small. Among shipped tokens, exactly one pair qualifies: Primer gray 4→5 (trained difference 0.019). The audit's smallest chromatic step, Material blue 400→500, measures 0.044 on a metric that saturates near 0.15 — clearly visible — so the build asserts it is rejected by the gate and shows it only as a receipt-chip pair inside Chapter 1.3. One other visual passes the gate: Chapter 5's hue-lock receipt (0.0121), a constructed equal-L/C pair rather than a shipped ramp step — and the generated red's pair (0.0297) sits just above the threshold, so it is denied the seamless treatment, which is the gate doing its job on our own palette too.

Chapter 5's column is only meaningful if it comes from the same math as the other six, so the build re-derives every scorecard scale with the replica pipeline and fails on any disagreement. Both the wins (hue lock < 3° and at least 4× tighter than the best audited system, ink-true gray, zero reversals, every adjacent step over the 0.025 JND gate, the chroma law within 0.025 on all three ramps) and the losses (4th of 7 on mean step CV behind Chakra, Primer and Bootstrap; 3rd on mean L-CV behind Bootstrap and Chakra; a 8.0× max/min cliff inside the generated blue; green-500 failing white text) are asserted — if a future helmlab version fixes a disclosed loss, the build fails until the text is updated, so the honesty cannot go stale in either direction.

Provenance & limitations

  • Token sources are the official npm packages, versions recorded in data/tokens/*.json. Tailwind v4 publishes oklch(), converted to sRGB hex via culori; Bootstrap's chromatic ramps are generated exactly per its own tint-color/shade-color (sass mix()) definitions.
  • This audit measures scale quality only — how evenly and predictably a ramp is spaced in a perceptual space. It says nothing about overall design quality, aesthetics, component design, or the fitness of these palettes for their intended workflows.
  • Radix Colors is role-based by design: its 12 steps are documented use-case roles, not an even ramp, so uniformity metrics measure a contract it never signed. Its Chapter 4 card carries the reframe.
  • Results depend on the choice of perceptual space; GenSpace correlates strongly with OKLab-class spaces, so orderings should be broadly stable, but exact numbers are space-specific.
  • This page checks itself at build time: every text/background pair in the design is verified against WCAG (body text ≥ 7:1 AAA, secondary text ≥ 4.5:1 AA) using helmlab's contrastRatio; every near-duplicate visual is verified against the honesty gate; and every headline claim above (largest/smallest step, gray tints, blue-500 spreads, contrast pass/fail verdicts, "what to steal" superlatives) is asserted against the data — the build fails otherwise.
